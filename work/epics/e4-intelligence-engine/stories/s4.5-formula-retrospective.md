## Summary

The portfolio is now ordered by expected recoverable pesos, and the subset holding 80% of that
value is identified. 48 new tests; backend 260 → 308.

The story's most useful output was not the formula, which is one multiplication. It was
discovering, before writing any of it, that **the data does not have the property the feature was
named after.**

## What went well

- **Measuring first killed a false premise.** "Pareto" invites 20/80. Measured: the top 20% of
  clients hold **40.1%** of expected recoverable value, and reaching 80% takes ~58% of the
  portfolio — consistent across all three sectors (39.9 / 40.1 / 40.5). Had this been assumed, the
  demo would have carried a claim the data contradicts.
- **Sequencing the contract ahead of the risky logic unblocked Nano within the hour.** He was
  waiting on the *shape*, not the algorithm. Pushing T1 before starting T2 also fixed the contract
  before the part most likely to change it — so any later change would have to be told to him
  rather than merged quietly.
- **AC #13 worked.** Checking for unused public APIs *before* close, rather than letting the AR
  gate find them, caught `subset_expected_recoverable`. It was kept and tested rather than deleted,
  because an endpoint genuinely needs "$57,000 of $69,190" — a deliberate decision instead of an
  accident. Three previous stories each shipped an unused API; this one did not.
- **Minimality was tested by construction.** `test_pareto_returns_smallest_qualifying_prefix`
  builds a portfolio where 80% needs exactly three of four clients, then proves minimality by
  removing the prefix's last member. Asserting the share alone would have passed for the entire
  portfolio.

## What to improve

- **The design's risk table said "80% adopted because Pareto says so" was a risk, and the
  mitigation was to measure — which happened. But the deeper question went unasked until
  measurement forced it:** *is this data shaped like the thing we are modelling at all?* The
  generator's normal amount distribution was visible in s3.1's code from the start.
- **`prioritize()` takes two dicts that must agree on their keys.** The mismatch raises loudly,
  which is right, but a single mapping of `client_id → (score, outstanding)` would make the
  invariant unrepresentable rather than merely checked. Not changed, because the two-dict shape
  matches what `ScoringRun` naturally exposes — a real trade, recorded rather than silently taken.
- **I read the queue and stopped at "it looks right".** Two observations that no test captures
  came out of it — the queue is dominated by medium/high scores, and a cheap-to-collect 83.6-score
  account sits at rank 271. Both are consequences of expected-value ranking, both are arguably
  wrong for a collections team, and neither is a bug. They should have been in the design's open
  questions, not discovered at T3.

## Heutagogical Checkpoint

1. **What did I learn?**
   That naming a feature after a statistical law is a claim requiring verification. "Pareto
   filtering" implies concentration; this portfolio has almost none, because `ProceduralGenerator`
   draws amounts from `Normal(10000, 3000)` — every client holds a similar balance, so the only
   source of spread is score variation. The filter is correct and the data is flat, which is a
   different problem than a wrong formula and needs a different fix.

2. **What would I change about the process?**
   Ask "does the data have the shape this feature assumes?" during design, as a standing question
   rather than an incidental one. I measured the *concentration curve* because the threshold
   needed justifying; I would not have measured it if the threshold had been uncontroversial, and
   the false premise would have shipped.

3. **Framework improvements?**
   - The design skill's risk table catches "is this number justified?" but not "is this
     phenomenon present?". For any feature named after a distribution or law — Pareto, power law,
     long tail, normal — the design should require evidence the data exhibits it.
   - Fourth story running with a note about structural/pre-close checks. AC #13 is a per-story
     workaround for something that wants to be a repo-level check.

4. **More capable of now?**
   Recognising when a metric has units and defending them. `outstanding × score/100` is pesos only
   because s4.3 refused `class_weight` to keep `predict_proba` calibrated — a decision made two
   stories earlier for a different reason entirely. Tracing that dependency backwards, and stating
   it in the module docstring so the next person does not casually break it, is the transferable
   skill.

## Improvements applied

- Concentration is reported as measured figures (`159 de 271 cuentas concentran 80.0%`), never as
  an inherited 20/80 claim.
- `test_top_twenty_percent_holds_far_less_than_eighty_percent` exists specifically to fail if
  anyone reintroduces that claim.
- The generator's flat amount distribution is parked with the measurement behind it, not fixed
  here — changing it invalidates seed-42 fixtures that s4.2, s4.3 and s4.4 tests pin.
- Threshold is a parameter carrying its measured curve in the docstring, so nobody reads 0.80 as
  a property of the data.

## Carried forward

- **The queue is dominated by medium and high scores.** A 30-score account needs a very large
  balance to outrank a 70-score one, so the *riskiest* accounts are not chased hardest. That is
  expected-value ranking doing exactly what it says, and it is the difference between "maximise
  recovery" and "prevent write-offs" — a policy question for Gustavo, not a formula change.
- **Rank 271 is an 83.6-score client owing $1,108.** Cheap to collect, permanently invisible.
  E5 workload policy.
- **The demo has no Pareto story until the generator produces heavy-tailed amounts.** Parked with
  the measurement; it needs its own story because of the fixture dependency.
- **s4.5-API (Nano) builds against `5d8980f`.** Nothing after T1 changed the contract.
